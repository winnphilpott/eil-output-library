# Edits — EPA Compliance Workshops Research Highlight

## Bottom Line

### Current

> **Are current Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) regulation enforcement policies helping minimize violations?** In the Journal of Policy Analysis and Management, Ferraro and Shimshack (2026) show that compliance assistance (i.e. offering informative workshops to polluting facilities to help them comply with regulations) has little to no effect on reducing violations.

**Issues:** Opens with a question, which defers the finding rather than leading with it. "Has little to no effect" is hedged and academic. Naming the journal in the body reads as citation scaffolding, not audience-facing prose. For a general interest reader, none of this answers "so what?"

---

### Suggested alternatives

**Option A — Lead with the policy stakes**

> **The EPA has quietly shifted away from fining polluters — and the replacement isn't working.** A new study finds that "compliance assistance" programs, where the agency offers free workshops to help facilities follow the rules, have zero effect on reducing violations. The decline in pollution that followed the workshops was already underway before they began.

*Why:* Grounds the finding in something a lay reader already has a stake in (EPA enforcement), names the mechanism ("workshops"), and ends on the key methodological insight without jargon.

---

**Option B — Lead with the finding as a concrete fact**

> **Offering polluters free help to follow environmental rules doesn't make them more likely to follow them.** Researchers studied thousands of facilities that received EPA compliance workshops and found no reduction in violations — the program looked effective at first glance, but the improvement was a pre-existing trend, not caused by the workshops.

*Why:* Shorter and blunter. "Offering polluters free help" is more vivid than "compliance assistance." Explains the core methodological point (pre-existing trend) in plain language.

---

**Option C — Lead with the implication for taxpayers/policy**

> **The EPA has been spending resources on a program that doesn't reduce pollution violations.** Free workshops offered to polluting facilities — the agency's preferred alternative to inspections and fines — have no measurable effect on compliance, according to a new study. Facilities that got the help did no better than those that turned it down.

*Why:* "Doesn't reduce pollution violations" is the clearest possible statement of the finding. The comparison to facilities that turned down help echoes the pull quote and makes the counterfactual concrete.

---

### Other suggestions

- **Background:** The phrase "deregulation is a helpful tool, or an ineffective alternative" is ambiguous — a general reader may not know compliance assistance *is* a form of deregulation. Worth making that explicit: something like "whether pulling back on penalties is a smart trade-off, or just letting polluters off the hook."
- **Implications — Funding column:** "programs need to actually work" is doing a lot of work without specificity. Could sharpen to something like: "With EPA enforcement budgets under pressure, shifting dollars to a program with no proven effect means fewer inspections — and potentially more pollution."
